India as a mega Biodiversity

Introduction of india as a mega diversity

  • India, with its diverse landscapes ranging from the Himalayas in the north to the tropical rainforests in the south, is recognized as one of the world's mega biodiversity hotspots. Its vast territory encompasses a rich variety of ecosystems, including forests, grasslands, wetlands, and coastal regions, which support an incredible array of flora and fauna.

  • With over 7% of the world's recorded species, India ranks among the top biodiversity-rich countries globally. Its unique geography and climate have given rise to numerous endemic species found nowhere else on Earth.

  • However, India's biodiversity faces numerous threats, including habitat loss, fragmentation, pollution, climate change, and overexploitation of natural resources. Conservation efforts are crucial to safeguarding India's rich biological heritage for future generations.

  • India has two major realms called the Palaearctic and the Indo- Malayan; and three biomes, namely the tropical humid forests, the tropical dry deciduous forests and the warm desert/semi-deserts.

Palaearctic Realm

  • The Palearctic or Palaearctic is one of the eight ecozones dividing the Earth surface.

  • Physically, the Palearctic is the largest ecozone. It includes the terrestrial ecoregions of Europe, Asia north of the Himalaya foothills, northern Africa, and the northern and central parts of the Arabian Peninsula.

  • The Palearctic or Palaearctic is the largest of the eight biogeographic realms of the Earth. It stretches across all of Eurasia north of the foothills of the Himalayas, and North Africa.

  • The realm consists of several bioregions: the Euro-Siberian region; the Mediterranean Basin; the Sahara and Arabian Deserts; and Western, Central and East Asia. The Palaearctic realm also has numerous rivers and lakes, forming several freshwater ecoregions.

  • The term 'Palearctic' was first used in the 19th century, and is still in use as the basis for zoogeographic classification.

Eastern Himalayas

  • The area comprises Nepal, Bhutan, and neighboring states of northern India, along with a continuous sector of the Yunnan province in Southwest china.

  • All Himalayan forests lie north of the Tropic of Cancer, and some of them are at altitudes of 1780 -3500 m, they can be considered tropical forests.

  • Many deep and semi isolated valleys are exceptionally rich in endemic plant species, In Sikkim, in an area of 7298 km2 , of the 4250 plant species , 2550 (60%) are endemic

  • In Nepal, there are around 7000 plant species, many of which overlap with those of India, Bhutan, and even Yunnan. Of these species, at least 500(8%) are believed to be endemic to Nepal.

Country of biodiversity

  • The title of "country of biodiversity" can be attributed to several nations, as many countries boast rich and diverse ecosystems. However, one prominent example is Brazil. Brazil is often referred to as the "country of biodiversity" due to the incredible variety of plant and animal species found within its borders. The Amazon rainforest, which spans a significant portion of Brazil, is one of the most biodiverse regions on the planet, housing countless species of plants, animals, insects, and microorganisms. Additionally, Brazil is home to other diverse ecosystems such as the Atlantic Forest, the Cerrado, and the Pantanal, further contributing to its reputation as a biodiversity hotspot.
